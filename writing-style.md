# Writing style guide

A personal guide for essays, posts, and newsletters. The goal is writing that sounds like a clear-thinking person talking, not like LinkedIn and not like AI. Most of this is a revision discipline, not a drafting one. Draft however you want. Then run these passes.

The order matters. Rhythm is the thing that most makes writing feel human, so it comes first.

---

## 1. Rhythm: vary sentence length on purpose

This is the single biggest lever. The tell of flat writing is that every sentence is the same length, usually 15 to 25 words. The ear flatlines and the reader skims.

The move: let a sentence run long and accumulate, then drop a short one on top of it. The short sentence only lands because the long one built a floor under it.

> "If 'tell them what to do and check in at key points' were all management required, people would not cry at work as often as they do. But I'll take the bait." (Hilary Gridley)

Twenty-six words, then five.

Closers especially want descending fragments before the final seal:

> "Bad context, applied at scale, all day long. A team that produces more but learns less. Speed pointed in the wrong direction. These are expensive problems, and they are all management problems."

**Drill:** read the draft out loud and mark a slash everywhere you take a breath. If the gaps are all the same size, that's the flatline. Go to your three best sentences and put a short, plain beat next to each.

A warning, see section 4: the short beats should sound like a person reacting ("But I'll take the bait," "Fine"), not like a copywriter's slogan ("It's the loop"). Conversational, not aphoristic.

---

## 2. Cut fear hedges. Keep breath words.

There are two kinds of hedge and they are not the same.

**Fear hedges** are you asking permission to make your point. Cut them.
- Before: "In practice, I think it takes some serious scrutiny to understand whether you are high agency or not."
- After: "I want to do the thing the job postings won't, and actually define it."

Words that usually signal a fear hedge: *I think, I believe, in practice, it could be argued, somewhat, fundamentally, arguably.*

**Breath words** are pacing, the verbal lean-back before you say the thing. Keep them. They make you sound like a person talking.
- "Well, managing people and managing agents have a lot in common."
- "Of course, the lines have always been blurry."
- "And here's the part that stuck with me."

The test is not "is this a hedge." It is: **am I softening the claim, or just breathing?** Soften = cut. Breathe = keep.

---

## 3. When you stack synonyms, you don't know what you mean yet

The most common LinkedIn-ism is three fuzzy near-synonyms in a row. The reader's eye slides off all of them, because none is a thing they can picture.

- Before: "take more ownership, be more proactive, jump into areas that need help, and make your manager's life easier"
- After: "Find the thing that's half-broken and that nobody owns, take it, get the people who care about it bought in, and ship something."

Rule: when you catch yourself listing several ways to say the same thing, stop and pick the one concrete instance. One image beats four abstractions. And whenever an abstraction shows up (ownership, proactive, drive, value, impact), ask "what would this look like as a scene?" and replace at least half of them with the scene.

---

## 4. The antithesis pair is an AI tell

Contrast is good. The specific *rhythm* of two same-length sentences glued by antithesis, in a clipped "Not X. Actually Y." cadence, is the tell, because models reach for it constantly.

- Tell: "That is not a description of software. That is a description of every good new hire."
- Tell: "The point is not to do more work. The point is to run the full loop."

Used once, deliberately, it's a punch. Used every third paragraph, a reader feels the machine. The fix is to dissolve it into one longer flowing sentence that surprises at the end, the way good contrast usually works in speech:

> "We wrote that down, carefully, for a piece of marketing software. We have never bothered to write it down for the people we hire, even though it describes them exactly."

Same contrast, no matched-pair cadence.

This is the most important AI-tell to watch for, but the family is bigger: matched-length sentence pairs of any kind, manufactured one-line "punch" slogans, and triads that are too clean all read as machine.

---

## 5. The negation test

For any sentence, ask: does anyone actually hold the opposite view? "Make your manager's life easier" passes nobody's test; no one argues against it, so it carries zero information even though it feels like a point.

If the opposite of your sentence is not a real position someone could hold, cut the sentence. What survives is the stuff with an edge.

---

## 6. End on the hardest specific, not the applause line

The inspirational closer is the LinkedIn reflex. It feels strong and is actually the weakest line, because it could end any piece.

- Applause line: "With a mix of curiosity and the drive to succeed, you will be unstoppable."
- Specific: "It becomes the truest line on your resume."

Rule: if your last sentence could be pasted onto a different essay, it is not your ending. Rewrite until only this piece could have written it.

---

## 7. Human moves worth stealing

These are what make writing feel like a person, not a brand account.

- **The unflattering aside.** Undercut yourself mid-point and you become trustworthy. "I mostly used ChatGPT to settle arguments and ask it dumb questions." "Maybe I am revealing something deeply sinister about myself."
- **Named, specific references.** Proper nouns are the opposite of LinkedIn. A real company, a real book title, a real number beats a category every time. Specificity is credibility.
- **The isolated line.** To emphasize something, give it its own paragraph instead of bolding it or reaching for punctuation. It does the work a dash would do, more quietly.
- **Clusters of rhetorical questions** for momentum: "How do I automate more of this? How do I cut the time on that? How do I make it more useful?" Good for a self-test or a building-energy passage.
- **Concrete questions over claims.** "Has anyone stopped checking your work lately?" makes the reader test the idea on their own life, which beats explaining it.

---

## 8. Mechanics

- **No em-dashes.** Use a comma, a period, a new sentence, or an isolated line (section 7). Em-dashes are also a mild AI tell.
- **UTM all outbound links** to owned properties so attribution is consistent.
- **Prioritize interesting over complete.** A piece does not have to cover everything. It has to be worth reading. Cut the dutiful paragraph.

---

## The revision pass, in order

Write the draft however it comes out, LinkedIn-isms and all. That's where the ideas live, so don't fight it on the first pass. Then run this top to bottom:

1. **Read it out loud and mark the breaths.** Fix the flatline first (section 1). This catches more than anything else.
2. **Delete fear hedges; keep breath words** (section 2).
3. **Find every synonym stack, pick the concrete one** (section 3).
4. **Hunt antithesis pairs and matched-length punches, dissolve them** (section 4).
5. **Negation-test each sentence; cut the ones with no opposite** (section 5).
6. **Replace abstractions with scenes** (section 3).
7. **Reread the last line. Could it end someone else's piece? Rewrite it** (section 6).
8. **Add one human move** if the piece feels too clean (section 7).

The ideas are the hard part, and they're usually already on the page after the first draft. This pass just wipes the fog off them.
